 1. Keywords: Standard length, fish measurement, caudal peduncle, damaged fins, deformed fins.
2. Abstract: The standard length method is a technique used to measure the length of a fish from the anterior-most extreme of its head to the posterior margin of its hypural bone, which is visible when flexing the caudal peduncle. This method is an alternative when the fish has damaged or deformed caudal fins, making it impossible to use other measurement methods such as maximum total length or fork length.
3. Description: The standard length method involves measuring the distance from the anterior-most extreme of the head (with jaw closed) to the posterior margin of the hypural bone, which is visible when flexing the caudal peduncle. This method should only be used when the fish specimen cannot be measured using the maximum total length method or the fork length method due to damaged or deformed caudal fins. The standard length method was developed by Anderson and Gutreuter (1983) as a reliable alternative for measuring fish length in such cases.
4. Target: To provide an accurate measurement of a fish's length when the maximum total length or fork length methods cannot be used due to damaged or deformed caudal fins.
5. Constraints: The standard length method can only be used when the fish specimen has damaged or deformed caudal fins, and it may not be applicable to all species of fish. Additionally, this method requires visual access to the hypural bone, which may not always be possible depending on the size and shape of the fish. Proper training and equipment are necessary for accurate measurements using this method.
