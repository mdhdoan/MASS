 1. Keywords: Streamflow, discharge, gaging stations, current-meter method, hydrographer.

2. Abstract: The measurement of streamflow, or discharge, is crucial for understanding water resources and management. Gaging stations are set up to monitor the volume rate of flow of water in a river or stream, which includes any substances suspended or dissolved in the water. These measurements help determine the discharge rating for the site, which may be influenced by various factors such as stage, slope, and rate of change of stage. The current-meter method is commonly used to measure discharge, where observations of width, depth, and velocity are taken at intervals in a cross section of the stream. This information is vital for water resource planning, flood forecasting, and environmental monitoring.

3. Description: Streamflow, also known as discharge, is defined as the volume rate of flow of water, including any substances suspended or dissolved in the water. The unit of measurement for discharge is typically cubic feet per second (cfs) or cubic meters per second (cms). At each gaging station, discharge measurements are made to determine the discharge rating for the site. Initially, these measurements are taken frequently and over a wide range of stage to define the station rating. After that, measurements are taken at periodic intervals, usually monthly, to verify the rating or to detect any changes in the rating caused by changes in stream channel conditions.

The current-meter method is the most commonly used technique for measuring discharge in streams. This method involves taking observations of width, depth, and velocity at intervals in a cross section of the stream while the hydrographer is wading or supported by a cableway, bridge, ice cover, or boat. A current meter is used to measure velocity, which is then used to calculate discharge.

4. Target: The primary focus of this method is to accurately measure streamflow or discharge at gaging stations for water resource planning, flood forecasting, and environmental monitoring.

5. Constraints: This method requires specialized equipment such as current meters and access to the stream for observations. It also requires trained personnel to perform the measurements and analyze the data. Additionally, safety is a concern when performing measurements in or near flowing water.
