 1. Keywords: Linkage disequilibrium (LD), genetic markers, populations, genetic linkage, recombination, mutation, genetic drift, non-random mating, population structure.
2. Abstract: Linkage disequilibrium (LD) is a fundamental concept in genetics, which refers to the non-random association of alleles at two or more loci. Estimation of LD level provides valuable information about genetic markers and populations under study. It can reveal insights into genetic linkage, selection, recombination rate, mutation rate, genetic drift, non-random mating, and population structure. Based on LD analysis, geneticists make informed decisions regarding marker retention and population inclusion in further studies.
3. Description: Linkage disequilibrium (LD) is a measure of the correlation between alleles at different loci. Geneticists are interested in estimating the level of linkage disequilibrium to understand various aspects of the genetic markers and populations being studied. When LD is detected between two loci across multiple populations, only the marker with the highest allele frequency is typically retained for further analysis. Conversely, when LD is observed between many pairs of loci within a single population, that population may be excluded from additional studies due to the assumption of equilibrium within populations for many statistical tests. High levels of LD can indicate genetic linkage or limited recombination between markers, while low LD suggests independent assortment of alleles and greater distances between markers. The presence of LD can also be influenced by factors such as mutation rate, genetic drift, non-random mating, and population structure.
4. Target: The primary focus of linkage disequilibrium analysis is to infer information about genetic markers and populations, guiding decisions on marker retention and population inclusion in subsequent research.
5. Constraints: Linkage disequilibrium estimation can be influenced by factors such as sample size, marker density, and the presence of genotyping errors. Additionally, the complexity of evolutionary forces acting on natural populations may introduce challenges in interpreting LD patterns accurately.
